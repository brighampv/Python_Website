Basic information on our project

In this analysis, we examined a dataset of chess games and their outcomes. We analyzed the factors leading to winning, as well as common behavior in games and in match-ups. We did this with the hope of finding ways to explain wins or find opportunities for more probable wins. We used only python programming, to create all of the visualizations on this website.

Summary of our Conclusions

Based upon this analysis, we can see that the rating system is not a perfect system to predict a winner. There are a number of confounding factors and a variety of game types. Upon playing a couple games of chess on the website, we discovered that the time limitation plays a large role in forcing people to resign rather than losing by running out of time. The platform will temporarily ban you if you do not resign before you run out of time.

The rating system is also flawed because of the high number of games that occur by people who have not played very many games. A more accurate way of representing rating would be by including a confidence interval. Those that start at a score of 1500 may have a huge confidence interval. As they begin to play more games against other players that have established ratings, the confidence interval of the player’s rating will decrease. This also would be a way of telling if a player begins cheating. If they begin to win against players outside of their confidence interval, they could be temporarily banned from the site.

By leveraging the opening moves data, we could also assign probabilities to winning to each player as the game progresses. Then, in the event of a draw or an out of time, rating points could be awarded based upon the progress of the game rather than a simple tie or win.

What we looked for

Wins vs. Color
White won 50% of the time, black won 45% of the time, and the last 5% of games ended in a draw.
Ratings vs. Turns
We have found no correlation between the rating of players and how long they spend playing each game.
Turns vs Victory Status - Automated
We used ANOVA analysis and found that all significant comparisons were significant.
Color of Winner vs Turns to Win
We found that if black wins it would be with 60 turns whereas for white it is with only 56 turns.
Expected Wins Based on Rating Difference
we found that while player rating is significant, 64% of games were expected or normal based on player rating, whereas 36% of games caused an upset.
Opening Move vs Turns to Win
While many other opening sequences lead to either shorter or longer games, the Hippopota sequence leads to an abnormally short game.
